Course Overview

Stars are the building blocks of the Universe. They drive the energetic and chemical evolution of galaxies, and the elements heavier than helium that make up the world around us were forged inside them. This course develops the physics of stars in the order an astronomer encounters them: first what we observe, then how we model the light that reaches us, and finally what is happening inside.

We begin with stellar observations — distances, magnitudes, colors, spectra, binaries, and the Hertzsprung–Russell diagram — and the parameters they yield. We then study stellar atmospheres and radiative transfer, turn to the equations of stellar structure (hydrostatic equilibrium, the equation of state, energy transport, and nuclear energy generation), and close with stellar evolution: the main sequence, post-main-sequence phases, and stellar remnants.

Required text: M. H. Pinsonneault & B. S. Ryden, Stellar Structure and Evolution (Cambridge University Press, 2023). Course Q&A is on Piazza.

Textbook Figure Code, Notebooks & Data

The authors have released Jupyter notebooks, Python code, and the data files for every original figure in the required text. They are a fine way to see how a figure was produced, to generate high-resolution versions for your own notes, or to use as a starting point for further exploration:

→ github.com/CambridgeUniversityPress/Stellar-Structure-and-Evolution

Many notebooks include pre-computed MESA model output and MIST isochrone tables, so the figures can be reproduced without a heavy local installation (released under a Creative Commons BY 4.0 license). Our one computational assignment uses MESA Web, the browser-based interface to MESA, which requires no installation.

Further Reading

Looking up terminology and getting oriented. When the jargon accumulates, these are good first stops:

  • COSMOS — the Swinburne Encyclopedia of Astronomy: clear, searchable definitions of terms and concepts.
  • NASA ADS: the search system for the astronomical literature — trace any result back to the original papers.

Digging deeper. Complementary texts and references, in roughly increasing depth:

  • B. Carroll & D. Ostlie, An Introduction to Modern Astrophysics — an accessible parallel treatment.
  • D. Prialnik, An Introduction to the Theory of Stellar Structure and Evolution — concise and physically motivated.
  • A. C. Phillips, The Physics of Stars — short and elegant, with an order-of-magnitude flavor.
  • C. Hansen, S. Kawaler & V. Trimble, Stellar Interiors — a step up, including pulsations and seismology.
  • R. Kippenhahn, A. Weigert & A. Weiss, Stellar Structure and Evolution — the classic graduate reference.
  • G. Rybicki & A. Lightman, Radiative Processes in Astrophysics — the standard reference for radiative transfer.
  • Annual Review of Astronomy & Astrophysics — authoritative review articles at the research frontier.
