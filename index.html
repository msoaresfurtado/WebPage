I am an astrophysicist studying stellar clusters, stars, exoplanets, and exomoons. To investigate open questions in these astrophysical subdomains, I draw insights from observational data (photometric and spectroscopic) and computational simulations. Planetary Engulfment Events
The final fate of a non-negligible fraction of planets is to be engulfed by their host stars. Our research focuses on identifying long-devoured planets via the observational signatures they impart upon the stars that consume them. More specifically, we identify statistically-significant chemical tracers, leveraging these signatures to place constraints on the bulk chemical composition and mass of the detsroyed planet. Our team also investigates the process of planetary disassociation, probing the transfer of angular momentum, energy, and mass during the engulfment process.

The Detection and Characterization of Young Exoplanets
Despite the discovery of over 5,000 exoplanets, young Earth-sized worlds remain a rarity. Those discovered offer key insights to build our understanding of rocky planet formation and evolution. Our team recently detected and characterized the nearest Earth-sized exoplanet orbiting a Sun-like star. This newly-discovered world is the third planet found in this system and it closely orbits its stellar host with a period of 4 days. Notably, the host star is a member of a 400 million year stellar association (10x younger than our own planet). Given the fact that the planet transits its host star, there is a valuable opportunity to further characterize this system via follow-up observational investigations. Our team is working to better understand the physical/atmospheric properties of this young world. We are also working to discover more young planets in nearby stellar associations.

The Search for Young Exomoons
Leveraging the infrared capabilities of the Nancy Grace Roman Space Telescope, it is possible to probe for transiting extraterrestrial bodies in young star-forming regions. One such region, the Orion Nebula Cluster (ONC), is a young and densely populated cluster, harboring a large number of infrared-bright brown dwarfs (BDs) and free-floating planets (FFPs). Our team has investigated the expected transit yields of a 30-day survey of the ONC. Preliminary findings suggest an expected discovery of approximately 14 (3-22) exomoons/satellites transiting FFPs and 54 (8-100) satellites transiting BDs. We aim to explore yields in other star-forming regions and to update our ONC predictions by incorporating the larger population of FFPs and BDs, as observed by the James Webb Space Telescope.
